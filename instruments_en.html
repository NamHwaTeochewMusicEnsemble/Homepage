Yangqin 扬琴

The Yangqin (hammered dulcimer) is a highly distinctive plucked instrument in Teochew music, frequently featured in ensembles such as Teochew string poetry and grand gong-and-drum performances. Its bright and penetrating tone allows it to lead melodies and reinforce rhythmic structures within the ensemble. With versatile playing techniques, the yangqin can express delicate lyrical passages as well as create a lively, festive atmosphere. As a result, it is regarded as one of the core instruments in Teochew music, embodying the region’s cultural essence and highlighting the richness and depth of its musical tradition.

Yehu 椰胡

The Yehu is a distinctive bowed string instrument in Teochew music, named after its resonator, which is often made from a coconut shell. Its tone is gentle yet resilient, making it a key carrier of melody and emotion within ensembles. The yehu excels at conveying the lyrical qualities of Teochew music while also blending seamlessly into more festive and dynamic settings. As an essential member of the Teochew string family, the yehu enriches the ensemble’s texture and embodies the regional character and cultural depth of Teochew music.

Erxian 二弦

The erxian is a highly representative bowed string instrument of the Teochew region. Originating from the xiqin of the Tang dynasty, it evolved through the jiqin of the Song and the lantou xian of the Ming, and by the early Qing period had taken its modern form with an ebony soundbox covered in python skin. Exquisitely crafted and often decorated with mother-of-pearl inlay, the erxian produces a bright, penetrating tone that is both crisp and expressive, capable of conveying sweet and graceful melodies with strong artistic appeal. In Teochew string poetry ensembles and opera, it frequently serves as the leading instrument, guiding rhythm and emotional expression. With the spread of Teochew culture, the erxian has reached Hong Kong, Macau, Southeast Asia, and even Europe and America, becoming an enduring symbol of Teochew music.

Erhu 二胡

The erhu, a traditional Chinese bowed string instrument, also plays a significant role in Teochew music. Known for its rich and expressive tone, the erhu conveys subtle emotions and dynamic melodic contours. In ensemble settings, it often carries the main melody or engages in dialogue with other instruments, capable of expressing both lyrical tenderness and passionate intensity. As a vital member of the Teochew string ensemble, the erhu enhances the overall texture and expands the expressive range of Teochew music.

Zhonghu 中胡

The zhonghu is a common bowed string instrument in Teochew music, with a range between the erhu and the dahu. Its tone is rich and mellow, serving as a bridge in the ensemble by supporting the harmonic foundation while responding to the main melody. As a central member of the Teochew string section, the zhonghu balances the ensemble’s texture and adds depth, enhancing the layered quality and expressive richness of Teochew music.

Tihu 提胡

The ti-hu is an important member of the Teochew bowed string family, named after the way it is held upright during performance. With its bright and penetrating tone, the ti-hu occupies the higher register and often carries melodic lines or interacts with other instruments in ensemble settings. It excels at expressing lyrical passages while also enhancing festive and energetic moments. As a vital part of Teochew string music, the ti-hu enriches the ensemble’s texture and highlights the agility and diversity of Teochew musical tradition.

Cello 大提琴

The cello, though originally a Western bowed string instrument, has been incorporated into Teochew ensembles to enrich their sound. With its wide range and deep, resonant tone, the cello often provides the bass line, stabilizing rhythm and harmonic structure. It complements traditional instruments while expanding the expressive possibilities of Teochew music, adding depth and gravitas. This integration allows the regional tradition to resonate with greater complexity and presence on modern stages.

Big & Small Suona 大小唢呐

Both the large suona and the small suona hold significant roles in Teochew music. The suona’s bright, piercing tone makes it ideal for conveying festive and exuberant atmospheres. The big suona, with its powerful volume and majestic timbre, often leads in grand gong-and-drum performances or ceremonial occasions, heightening the sense of grandeur. The small suona, with a relatively softer tone, is better suited for indoor settings or lyrical pieces, blending smoothly with string instruments. Together, they complement each other, enriching the ensemble’s texture and showcasing the diversity and expressive range of Teochew music.

Pipa 琵琶

The pipa, one of the most iconic traditional Chinese plucked instruments, also plays a significant role in Teochew music. Its crisp and layered tone allows it to convey delicate lyrical melodies as well as vigorous rhythmic passages. With a wide range of techniques such as tremolo, strumming, and bending, the pipa brings great expressive variety to the ensemble. In Teochew music performances, it often serves as a bridge between bowed strings and percussion, enriching the overall texture and balance. The pipa is not only a link between melody and rhythm but also a vital instrument that embodies classical elegance and regional character within Teochew music.

Zhongruan 中阮

The zhongruan is a traditional Chinese plucked instrument, also widely used in Teochew music. Similar in appearance to the pipa, it produces a fuller and mellower tone, occupying the middle register and often serving as a bridge within the ensemble. The zhongruan can provide harmonic support as well as carry melodic lines, with its warm and resonant sound adding depth and richness to Teochew music. Its versatile techniques allow it to express subtle emotions in lyrical passages and enhance the festive atmosphere in lively sections, making it an indispensable member of the Teochew string ensemble.

Teochew Drum 潮州鼓

The Teochew drum is one of the most powerful percussion instruments in Teochew music, frequently featured in ensembles such as grand gong-and-drum performances and Teochew string poetry. With its deep and resonant tone, the drum provides a strong rhythmic foundation and enhances the overall atmosphere. In large-scale settings, it often takes a leading role, driving the tempo and emotional intensity of the ensemble; in more lyrical passages, its subtle beats add texture and nuance. The Teochew drum is not only the rhythmic core but also a symbol of strength and solemnity in Teochew music.

Large and Small Cymbals 大小钹

The large cymbals and small cymbals (also known as da-cha, xiao-cha) are distinctive percussion instruments in Teochew music. Made of paired metal discs struck together, they produce resonant or crisp sounds depending on size and playing style. The large cymbals deliver powerful, majestic tones, often used to highlight dramatic moments or reinforce strong rhythmic accents. The small cymbals, with their bright and agile timbre, are ideal for embellishing delicate passages or interacting with other instruments. Together, they play complementary roles in grand gong-and-drum ensembles and Teochew orchestras, enriching the texture and showcasing the unique rhythmic vitality of Teochew music.

The Hanging Cymbal 吊钹

The hanging cymbal (diao-bo， also know as K cymbal) is a percussion instrument commonly used in Teochew music, derived from the broader family of cymbals. Typically played in a suspended position, it produces a crisp and lingering tone that serves as an embellishment within the ensemble. Unlike the large cymbals’ powerful resonance or the small cymbals’ bright agility, the hanging cymbal is distinguished by its sustained sound, often marking transitions or enhancing atmosphere. In grand gong-and-drum performances and ensemble settings, it plays a supportive role, adding depth and spatial texture to the overall sound, making it an essential rhythmic element in Teochew music.

Kim Gia 钦仔

The Kim Gia (or qin-zai) is a distinctive small percussion instrument in Teochew music, often featured in grand gong-and-drum ensembles and festive folk occasions. Resembling a small gong, it produces a crisp and brief tone, typically used to signal rhythmic transitions or highlight structural changes in the music. Though modest in volume, its sound is highly recognizable and serves as a guiding accent within the ensemble. Frequently paired with larger gongs and cymbals, the qin-zai adds clarity and rhythmic definition, making it an indispensable “cueing” instrument in Teochew musical tradition.

Yue-luo, Jiu-zai-luo 月锣、九仔锣

The yue-luo (moon gong) and jiu-zai-luo (small gong) are traditional gongs frequently used in Teochew music, each with distinct tonal qualities. The moon gong, being larger, produces a deep and sustained resonance, often employed to create solemn atmospheres or mark transitions. The small gong, by contrast, has a crisp and brief tone, typically used to signal rhythmic changes or embellish ensemble passages. Together, they complement each other in grand gong-and-drum performances and Teochew ensembles, enhancing both the grandeur and rhythmic texture of the music. These instruments serve not only as vital rhythmic anchors but also as expressive symbols of Teochew's unique musical character.

Shenbo 深波

The shenbo is a distinctive gong-type percussion instrument in Teochew music. Larger in size, it produces a deep and resonant tone, often used to create solemn and dignified atmospheres. Its powerful resonance provides rhythmic stability and highlights structural transitions within the ensemble. Frequently paired with large gongs and cymbals, the shenbo plays a crucial role in grand gong-and-drum performances and ceremonial contexts. More than just a rhythmic anchor, it symbolizes strength and gravity, making it an emblematic instrument in Teochew musical tradition.

Suluo 苏锣

The su-luo in Teochew music refers to the traditional Chinese large gong. With its sizeable frame and deep, resonant tone, it plays a leading role in ensembles, especially in grand gong-and-drum performances and ceremonial contexts. The su-luo is often struck to mark the beginning of a piece or highlight major transitions, serving as a guiding and commanding presence. Its powerful resonance not only anchors the rhythm but also symbolizes solemnity and strength, making it one of the most emblematic instruments in Teochew music.

Bronze Bell 铜钟

The bronze bell (tong-zhong) is a rare yet solemn percussion instrument in Teochew music, often reserved for ceremonial or large-scale occasions. Cast in bronze, it produces a deep, resonant tone with powerful reverberation. Striking the bell not only stabilizes rhythm but also creates a dignified and majestic atmosphere, frequently used at the beginning of pieces or during significant transitions. In grand gong-and-drum ensembles, as well as in rituals and festive ceremonies, the bronze bell symbolizes strength and sanctity, serving as an instrument that embodies cultural meaning within Teochew music.

Zhanluo 战锣

The zhan-luo, or “battle gong,” is one of the most powerful gong-type percussion instruments in Teochew music, named for its bold and forceful sound. Large in size, it produces a deep and commanding tone, often used to depict intense or dramatic moments and to highlight rhythmic transitions or surges in energy within the ensemble. Its striking carries a strong sense of ritual and theatricality, instantly capturing the audience’s attention. In grand gong-and-drum performances and festive occasions, the battle gong serves not only as a rhythmic anchor but also as a vital instrument for amplifying atmosphere and showcasing strength.
